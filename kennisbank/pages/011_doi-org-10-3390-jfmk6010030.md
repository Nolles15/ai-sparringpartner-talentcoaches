# Relationships between Workload, Heart Rate Variability, and Performance in a Recreational Endurance Runner

Bron: https://doi.org/10.3390/jfmk6010030
Opgehaald: 2026-06-18

---

Authors: Daniel Boullosa, André R. Medeiros, Andrew A. Flatt, Michael R. Esco, Fabio Y. Nakamura, Carl Foster

Published: Journal of Functional Morphology and Kinesiology, 2021, 6(1), 30. Case Report. Open Access (CC BY).

## Abstract (paraphrased)

This case report examines the relationship between heart rate variability (HRV), training load, and performance in a 50-year-old recreational female endurance runner over a 20-week competitive macrocycle, split into a first (M1) and second (M2) 10-week mesocycle, during which she set personal-best-adjacent times in 10 km and 21 km races. The researchers tracked workload (weekly distance, session RPE-based training load, and "monotony" — the ratio of mean to standard deviation of weekly load) alongside daily HRV indices (RMSSD, its coefficient of variation, and the RMSSD:RR ratio). In M2, RMSSD and RMSSD:RR both rose significantly while average RR interval length fell significantly. Monotony correlated with weekly volume, with RR, and inversely with RMSSD:RR. A sharp one-day drop in RMSSD preceded her first flu symptoms during the macrocycle. The authors conclude that combined HRV and sRPE monitoring is practical for recreational runners, that the RMSSD:RR ratio reflects meaningful training adaptations, and that high training volume combined with high monotony may be counterproductive — recommending mesocycle-level pattern tracking for better individualized periodization.

## Background and rationale

HRV (particularly RMSSD, a vagal-tone marker) is widely promoted as a tool for monitoring training in recreational runners — helping with method selection, daily adjustments, and spotting positive or negative adaptations. The RMSSD:RR ratio has been proposed in prior elite-athlete studies as an indicator of combined vagal-sympathetic ("vagal saturation") status. While links between performance and autonomic markers, or between performance and training-load markers, have each been studied separately, the authors note a lack of research connecting weekly training workload directly to weekly autonomic (HRV) changes in recreational athletes — motivating this case study, with an a priori hypothesis that lower RMSSD and RMSSD:RR would precede the runner's best performances (based on patterns reported in elite triathletes and rowers).

## Methods

The subject was a 50-year-old, 1.59 m, ~50–52 kg recreational runner (VO2max 56 mL/kg/min) with over a decade of endurance training history, racing two 10 km and three 21 km road events in Brasilia under thermoneutral morning conditions. Daily distance was GPS-tracked; session RPE, derived training load, and monotony were computed weekly. Her typical week mixed strength sessions, uphill treadmill running, cruise-pace intervals, occasional max-aerobic-speed intervals, easy recovery runs/walks with short maximal strides, and one long easy run, following a "polarized" intensity distribution (roughly 75–80% low intensity). Morning resting HRV (2-minute recordings, supine, post-waking) was captured via a Polar H7 chest strap and the Elite HRV app, from which weekly RMSSD, RMSSDcv, and RMSSD:RR were derived. Statistical comparisons between M1 and M2 used an unpaired t-test with Cohen's d, plus Pearson correlations between workload and HRV variables (significance at p<0.05).

## Results

Between M1 and M2, RMSSD increased from about 47.7 to 54.1 ms (p=0.006), the RMSSD:RR ratio increased from about 37.4 to 43.7 ms/s (p=0.002), and average RR interval length decreased from about 1.28 to 1.24 s (p=0.017); RMSSDcv, distance, training load, and monotony did not differ significantly between mesocycles, though effect sizes suggested M1 was the more demanding block. Weekly distance correlated with training load (r=0.738) and with monotony (r=0.552). Monotony also correlated with RR (r=0.447) and inversely with RMSSD:RR (r=-0.458). RMSSD:RR was strongly correlated with RMSSD itself (r=0.973) and with RMSSDcv (r=0.526), and inversely with RR (r=-0.581). A sudden RMSSD decline (from about 40 ms to 24 ms) occurred in week 8, one day before the onset of flu symptoms, followed by two days of disrupted training. Her best 10 km performance (week 12, 39:56, ~99.3% of personal best) coincided with peak weekly volume reached three weeks pre-race and very low monotony scores during the final two taper weeks.

## Discussion and conclusions

The authors highlight the novel finding that monotony correlates with both RR and the RMSSD:RR ratio, suggesting that highly monotonous training (even at moderate absolute load) may impair favorable autonomic adaptation, and that volume should be evaluated alongside monotony trends across multiple weeks rather than in isolation. Their hypothesis was only partly confirmed: while reduced RR aligned with better performance as expected, RMSSD:RR was actually higher (not lower) during the better-performing mesocycle, contrasting with some prior elite-athlete case studies but consistent with others — a discrepancy attributed to differences in recording protocols, periodization style, and sport demands. They argue RMSSD:RR plus RR together provide useful complementary information to RMSSD alone. The pre-illness RMSSD drop is flagged as a potentially useful early-warning signal for impending viral illness, worth further study. Limitations include the single-subject design, the runner's individualized "evolutionary periodization" approach (incorporating lifestyle, sleep, and nutrition adjustments), and potential sensitivity of results to the specific HRV recording protocol, device, and app used. Practical recommendation: recreational runners and coaches should track daily sRPE, RMSSD, and RR, and compute weekly training load, monotony, and RMSSD:RR to better individualize periodization relative to volume and performance trends.
