## Abstract

Athlete development depends on many factors that need to be balanced by the coach. The amount of data collected grows with the development of sensor technology. To make data-informed decisions for training prescription of their athletes, coaches could be supported by feedback through a coach dashboard. The aim of this paper is to describe the design of a coach dashboard based on scientific knowledge, user requirements, and (sensor) data to support decision making of coaches for athlete development in cyclic sports. The design process involved collaboration with coaches, embedded scientists, researchers, and IT professionals. A classic design thinking process was used to structure the research activities in five phases: empathise, define, ideate, prototype, and test phases. To understand the user requirements of coaches, a survey (n = 38), interviews (n = 8) and focus-group sessions (n = 4) were held. Design principles were adopted into mock-ups, prototypes, and the final coach dashboard. Designing a coach dashboard using the co-operative research design helped to gain deep insights into the specific user requirements of coaches in their daily training practice. Integrating these requirements, scientific knowledge, and functionalities in the final coach dashboard allows the coach to make data-informed decisions on training prescription and optimise athlete development.

Keywords: dashboard, training load, coach, feedback, athlete development, performance, overreaching, overtraining

## 1. Introduction

In their ambition to become elite athletes, the development of young athletes is crucial and depends on many factors that must be balanced. These factors are categorised as training load, physical performance, mental health, and daily life (e.g., school and social) factors. Coaches need to manage these factors to guide young athletes in becoming elite athletes and prevent non-functional overreaching, overtraining, and injuries. In order to make appropriate decisions for athlete development, a considerable amount of training data (e.g., heart rate, velocity) and recovery data (e.g., sleep, muscle soreness, perceived exertion) is collected. However, collecting, processing, and interpreting all these data can be difficult and time-consuming for the coach, even though this has potential benefits for the coach.

Cyclic sports (e.g., speed skating, cycling, and running) are characterised by repetitive activities and have a major focus on physiological aspects. Quantification of physiological aspects can be performed using internal and external training-load measures and monitoring. External load refers to the output performed by the athlete and internal load is the athlete's mental and physiological response to that output.

This descriptive research paper describes the process of designing a coach dashboard based on scientific knowledge, user requirements, and (sensor) data that supports coaches in decision making about training prescription for athlete development in cyclic sports, involving coaches, embedded scientists, researchers, and IT professionals.

## 2. Materials and Methods

The co-operative research design was part of the "Coach in Control" project, involving coaches, software engineers and data scientists from the Sport Data Valley platform, an innovation lab manager, and human-movement scientists. Stakeholders helping implement the dashboard were national sports associations (NOC*NSF, KNSB) and elite sports networks. A classic design thinking process structured the research in five phases: empathise, define, ideate, prototype, and test.

Target users were coaches working with talented speed skaters and runners, coaching performance-driven groups of young athletes without additional support from embedded/data scientists.

**2.1 Empathise phase** — A survey (based on a literature review on training/overreaching/overtraining monitoring) identified 48 factors quantifying training load, physical performance, mental health, and daily life. It was sent to 200 selected coaches (EQF level 4) via NOC*NSF and 6 national sports associations (speed skating, running, rowing, swimming, cycling, triathlon), later spread via social media. Eight follow-up interviews were held with elite-level coaches.

**2.2 Define phase** — Survey/interview results fed four focus-group sessions (4 participants each: a talent coach, elite coach, human-movement scientist, IT specialist), using the COMMONS tangible board game (adapted to a digital format for online MS Teams sessions due to COVID-19) to rank 30 statements.

**2.3 Ideate phase** — Two iterations of mock-ups were drawn and discussed in focus groups (3 then 5 coaches), scored on functionalities, content, design, overview, simplicity, and insights.

**2.4 Prototype phase** — Built using Agile Scrum on the existing Sport Data Valley platform infrastructure (daily questionnaires, training log questionnaire, sensor integrations with Polar/Garmin/Strava/Fitbit, athlete-coach data sharing). After four sprints, showcased to three human-movement scientists and three elite coaches.

**2.5 Test phase** — Four coaches used the minimum viable product for 6–8 weeks (two with 1–4 athletes, two with full groups of 12 and 14 athletes), followed by 30–45 min structured interviews.

## 3. Results

**3.1 Empathise** — 38 coaches returned the survey; 8 interviews held. No significant differences in importance of training data (4.0), performance testing data (3.9), and competition data (3.8) collection. Monitoring before training was rated most important (3.9); during training least important (3.3). Importance vs. actual use differed significantly for most categories. Most important pre-training measures: injuries (4.6), fatigue (4.4), recovery (4.4). During training: technical execution (4.4), heart rate (3.8), power (3.7). After training: experienced fun (4.3), perceived load (3.8), training duration (3.6). Most-cited bottlenecks: insufficient time to collect (61%), process (58%), and analyse (42%) data; one-third cited insufficient money/hardware/software. 60% of coaches used 2–5 different monitoring systems (own-built systems plus TrainingPeaks, Garmin Connect, Polar Flow, Strava). Most coaches wanted to spend <10 min before training, <5 min during, 6–20 min after training on monitoring; 80% wanted athletes to log data in ≤10 min/day. Top-rated functionalities: "select myself which measures to see," "short navigation from overview panel," "design my own overview page." Top feedback principles: "load of individual athlete per week," "detailed feedback on individual data," "load of individual athlete per session."

**3.2 Define** — Highest-ranked statement across four focus groups: "In a maximum of 20 min the coach should be able to use all individual training monitoring for the next training session," followed by warnings for reported injuries and large individual deviations. Feedback should combine external load, internal load, and subjective data, support but not prescribe decisions.

**3.3 Ideate** — Resulted in a two-page mock-up: a Health and Well-being page (daily health/well-being/training readiness overview) and a Training and Trends page (training measures and trends over time). Functionalities (4.0), content (4.2), and insights (3.9) scored highest; simplicity scored lowest.

**3.4 Prototype** — Took 20 weeks. Health and Well-being page implemented per mock-up (individual well-being warnings dropped for budget reasons). Training and Trends page split into a left panel (detailed single-activity data: training type, RPE, sRPE, duration, load, velocity, power, heart rate, fastest lap, heart-rate zones, training-measure graph) and a right panel (weekly trends: training load, Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio, well-being, resting heart rate, sleep duration). 113 specifications were prioritised and complexity-scored by the scrum team; page-load time for athletes with large data volumes was solved by loading only the most recent weeks by default.

**3.5 Test** — Four coaches tested the dashboard with 31 athletes total. Once coach-athlete connections were made, the dashboard provided relevant information; page-load-time feedback persisted; coaches needed ongoing onboarding support in the first two weeks. Combining questionnaire, training-log, and sensor data on one dashboard was seen as very helpful and time-saving.

## 4. Discussion

The co-operative research design gave deep insight into coaches' needs and bottlenecks (limited analysis time, juggling many systems). Online focus groups during COVID-19 worked well with strict discussion rules. Building on the existing Sport Data Valley infrastructure let the team focus on combining data sources into new dashboard views. A small Agile Scrum team (one developer, one human-movement scientist) was kept on track via weekly meetings and consultation with coaches/IT professionals. Because all coaches in the study worked in cyclic individual sports (speed skating, cycling, running), the results may not directly generalise to other sports, though internal-load and subjective measures are likely more transferable than sport-specific external-load measures. Added features in this study were velocity and power training load (average × duration), giving coaches "more information with fewer data."

## 5. Conclusions

The co-created Coach in Control dashboard let coaches make data-informed decisions, addressing time constraints and the burden of using multiple platforms, by covering the athlete monitoring cycle (training load, physical performance, mental health, daily life) within one dashboard with time-efficient, easy-to-understand functionality fitting coaches' existing workflow.

Limitations: effects of dashboard use on coach/athlete performance outcomes are not yet established; the focus on cyclic sports limits generalisability; only a subset of possible monitoring indicators was included.

Future work: validate the velocity/power load calculations against existing methods and internal-load measures; study the relationship between dashboard use and athletes' performance development and coach workflow; explore how the dashboard can support coach-athlete communication.
