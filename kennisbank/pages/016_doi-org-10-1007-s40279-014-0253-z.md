# Monitoring Training Load to Understand Fatigue in Athletes

Opgehaald: 2026-06-18

Sports Medicine (2014) 44(Suppl 2):S139-S147
Shona L. Halson
Open Access

Abstract
Training load and fatigue monitoring are important steps in the process of designing training programs for athletes, given the complex nature of the relationship between training and performance. Appropriate load monitoring can help to evaluate the level of fatigue and potentially the risk of illness and injury. Many different methods are now used to monitor training load and fatigue, ranging from subjective self-report measures of well-being, to biochemical and endocrine markers, neuromuscular function, and heart rate variability. Self-report measures of well-being and fatigue offer simplicity and low cost, while biochemical, hormonal, and neuromuscular measures provide a more objective assessment but can be time-consuming and expensive. The relationships between training load and measures of fatigue and well-being can provide useful information for athletes and coaches and may help to optimize training and recovery strategies.

1. Background
Athletes undertake training to enhance performance. The training process involves prescribing an appropriate amount of stress to stimulate adaptation, while avoiding excessive fatigue that could lead to a decrease in performance, illness, or injury. The monitoring of training load and the athlete's response to that load (i.e., fatigue) is therefore central to effective training prescription.

2. Training Load
Training load can be quantified using external measures (e.g., distance covered, power output) and internal measures (e.g., heart rate, perceived exertion). The interaction between external and internal load is influenced by individual characteristics including fitness, genetics, and prior training history.

3. Fatigue
Fatigue is a complex, multifactorial phenomenon that can manifest at the level of the central nervous system, the neuromuscular system, and at a psychological level. Acute fatigue resulting from a single training session is a normal and expected response, whereas chronic fatigue accumulated over multiple sessions without adequate recovery may indicate maladaptation or overtraining.

4. Subjective Measures of Fatigue and Well-Being
Self-report questionnaires (e.g., Profile of Mood States, Daily Analysis of Life Demands for Athletes, and customized wellness questionnaires) are widely used due to their low cost, ease of administration, and sensitivity to changes in athlete wellbeing.

5. Heart Rate-Based Measures
Heart rate variability (HRV) and heart rate recovery have been proposed as non-invasive indicators of autonomic nervous system status and may reflect changes in training load and fatigue.

6. Biochemical and Hormonal Markers
Markers such as creatine kinase, cortisol, testosterone, and the testosterone:cortisol ratio have been investigated as indicators of training stress, though their utility is often limited by high inter-individual variability and the cost and invasiveness of testing.

7. Immune Function
Heavy training loads have been associated with alterations in immune function, including changes in salivary IgA, which may relate to increased susceptibility to upper respiratory tract illness.

8. Neuromuscular Function
Measures such as countermovement jump performance have been used as indicators of neuromuscular fatigue and readiness to train or compete.

9. Sleep
Sleep quantity and quality are important components of recovery, and disturbances in sleep can both result from and contribute to increased training load and fatigue.

10. Practical Applications
A combination of monitoring tools, tailored to the constraints and needs of the sport and athlete group, is recommended. Simple, low-cost subjective measures can be used frequently, while more resource-intensive objective measures might be used periodically to corroborate subjective findings.

11. Limitations
Many monitoring tools have only been validated in specific populations or contexts, and findings may not generalize across sports, sexes, or competition levels. Individual variability also limits the use of generic thresholds for what constitutes a meaningful change.

12. Conclusions
Effective load and fatigue monitoring requires a multi-disciplinary, individualized approach combining subjective and objective measures. Such monitoring can support coaches and practitioners in making informed decisions about training prescription and recovery to optimize performance while minimizing the risk of illness, injury, and non-functional overreaching.
